What Big Open Data Reveal About the Cultural Evolution

by Nikola Sander, Vienna Institute of Demography

A new study by Maximilian Schich and colleagues published in Science on August 1 traces the cultural evolution in Europe and North America over the last two centuries. The authors draw on three databases (Freebase and two datasets on artists) to map the locations of birth and death of more than 150,000 notable people.

I am intrigued by their findings and hopeful that our interactive circular visualisation of the Freebase dataset, which the authors kindly made available with their paper, gives a complementary view of the cultural evolution across space and time.

One Big Open Dataset - Two Visualisations

Maximilian Schich and his colleagues visualise the cultural evolution using network flow maps. The maps in their article titled "A network framework of cultural history" show a very large number of arcs and nodes, making it difficult to tease out the main corridors of migration from place of birth to place of death and how they changed over time.

The animated visualisation of the cultural network shown in the video below by nature.com works very well. The narrated video tells an excellent story of how culture evolved as notable people moved across the globe. The visual complexity is reduced by effective narration and a focus on one region at a time.

The interactive circular plot we had originally developed for visualising global migration flows allow the cultural evolution to be viewed from a different angle. Both visualisations have their advantages and disadvantages. Network flow maps give a clear sense of location and direction, and changes over time can be revealed effectively using animation. But it can be difficult to visualise complex and dynamic networks without overwhelming the map.

Circular plots cannot provide the locational detail of an (animated) network map, but they effectively summarise all flows in the system and allow direct comparisons of the sizes of flows. The large size of flows within the United States in the 20th century may point to bias and incompleteness of the Freebase dataset. A compromise we had to make so as to allow direct comparisons of flows over time was to use a 2012 geography when identifying the country that each location (identified in the Freebase data using latitute and longitude) falls into.
